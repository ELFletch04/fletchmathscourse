Understanding Surds

✓

Surd: A surd is an irrational number in the form \( \sqrt{a} \) where \( a \) is not a square number.

Example: \( \sqrt{6} \) is a surd because 6 is not a square number.

✗

Not a Surd: If the number under the root is a square number, the expression is not considered a surd.

Example: \( \sqrt{4} \) is not a surd, as 4 is a square number. \( \sqrt{4} \) simplifies to 2.

Which of the following is not a surd?

A. \( \sqrt{16} \)
B. \( \sqrt{6} \)
C. \( \sqrt{19} \)
D. \( \sqrt{8} \)

In addition to understanding surds, you need to know how to apply basic arithmetic functions and how to simplify them effectively.


1. Multiplication

To multiply two surds together, you can simply find the product of the content of the surds and square root the result, like so:

$${\sqrt{a}\times\sqrt{b} = \sqrt{ab}}$$

For example:\[{\sqrt{5}}\times{\sqrt{6}}=\sqrt{30}\]

If the surds have any coefficients, you can just multiply those together too:\[{3\sqrt{2}}\times{5\sqrt{7}}=15\sqrt{14}\]

2. Division

To divide one surd by another, you can simply divide the content of the surds and square root the result, like so:

$${\frac{\sqrt{a}}{\sqrt{b}}=\sqrt{\frac{a}{b}} }$$

For example:

$${\frac{\sqrt{14}}{\sqrt{7}}=\sqrt{2} }$$

Similar to multiplication, if the surds have any coefficients you can just divide those too:\[\frac{12\sqrt{6}}{6\sqrt{3}}=2\sqrt{2} \]

To understand how to add and subtract surds, you first need to understand how to simplify surds, using the rules you just learnt.

To simplify a surd essentialy means to take a factor out of the surd to make the number inside the root as small as possible.


Simplifying Surds:

Take \( \sqrt{40} \) for example.

\( \sqrt{40} \) can be simplified by taking out a factor of \( \sqrt{4} \), so it becomes \( \sqrt{4}\times\sqrt{10} \)

We know that \( \sqrt{4} = 2 \)

so \( \sqrt{40}=2\sqrt{10} \)

Let's test your understanding with a quick question:


Which of the following simplifies to \( 5\sqrt{3} \)?

A. \( \sqrt{25} \)
B. \( \sqrt{45} \)
C. \( \sqrt{15} \)
D. \( \sqrt{75} \)

Now let's move on to adding and subtracting surds.

3. Addition

To add two surd expressions, they must share a common surd, similar to how fractions need a common denominator for addition.

Let's do an example, it is very simple you'll see.

$${3\sqrt{2}+4\sqrt{2}=7\sqrt{2} }$$

All you have to do is keep the surd the same, and add the numbers before the surd together.

Let's do another example.

$${\sqrt{5}+2\sqrt{5}=3\sqrt{5}}$$

4. Subtraction

Subtraction of surds is essentially the same as addition of surds, just with a - instead of a +.

For example:

$${4\sqrt{5}-\sqrt{5}=3\sqrt{5}}$$

Or:

$${10\sqrt{2}-6\sqrt{2}=4\sqrt{2}}$$

Which of the following is correct?

\(9\sqrt{5}+4\sqrt{5}=?\)

A. \(2 \sqrt{9} \)
B. \( 13\sqrt{5} \)
C. \( 5\sqrt{13} \)
D. \( 13\sqrt{25} \)

Which of the following is correct?

\(3\sqrt{40}+4\sqrt{10}=?\)

A. \( 10\sqrt{10} \)
B. \( 7\sqrt{50} \)
C. \( 7\sqrt{10} \)
D. \( 19\sqrt{40} \)

Expanding and Simplifying Double Brackets with Surds

When dealing with surds (square roots), expanding and simplifying double brackets involves using algebraic identities and properties of surds. Here's how you can do it:

Note: In the methods below, we've used a \(\cdot\) instead of a '\(\times\)' to maintain clarity and avoid clutter. Both symbols represent multiplication, but using \(\cdot\) can help keep your work neat and save time. Consider adopting \(\cdot\) in your own calculations for a cleaner presentation!

1. Expanding Double Brackets

To expand double brackets involving surds, use the distributive property. For example, to expand \( (a + \sqrt{b})(c + \sqrt{d}) \):

1. Multiply each term in the first bracket by each term in the second bracket:

\[ (a + \sqrt{b})(c + \sqrt{d}) = a \cdot c + a \cdot \sqrt{d} \] \[+ \sqrt{b} \cdot c + \sqrt{b} \cdot \sqrt{d} \]

2. Simplify the terms involving surds:

\[ \sqrt{b} \cdot \sqrt{d} = \sqrt{b \cdot d} \]

So the expanded form is:

\[ ac + a\sqrt{d} + c\sqrt{b} + \sqrt{b \cdot d} \]

2. Simplifying Expressions with Surds

To simplify expressions with surds, combine like terms and simplify the surds if possible. For example, simplify:

\[ (2 + \sqrt{3})(2 - \sqrt{3}) \]

1. Use the difference of squares formula (from GCSE):

\[ (a + \sqrt{b})(a - \sqrt{b}) = a^2 - b \]

2. Apply this to the expression:

\[ (2 + \sqrt{3})(2 - \sqrt{3}) = 2^2 - (\sqrt{3})^2 = 4 - 3 = 1 \]

3. Example Problems

Let's solve a problem to see how it works:

Expand and simplify:

\[ (3 + \sqrt{2})(4 - \sqrt{5}) \]

1. Multiply each term in the first bracket by each term in the second bracket:

\[ (3 + \sqrt{2})(4 - \sqrt{5}) = 3 \cdot 4 + 3 \cdot (-\sqrt{5}) \] \[+ \sqrt{2} \cdot 4 + \sqrt{2} \cdot (-\sqrt{5})\]

2. Simplify the terms involving surds:

\[ \sqrt{2} \cdot (-\sqrt{5}) = -\sqrt{2 \times 5} = -\sqrt{10} \]

So the simplified form is:

\[ 12 - 3\sqrt{5} + 4\sqrt{2} - \sqrt{10} \]

There is a good chance you know most of the above rules from GCSE maths, but let's move onto something new: Rationalising denominators.

Trust me, it's just as exciting as it sounds.



Rationalising the denominator:

Fractions that have a surd on the bottom are sort of frowned upon in the maths community. It makes them difficult to compare with other fractions, makes them less consistent with one another, and generally just makes them look a little bit ugly.

To address this, we use a technique known as rationalising the denominator. This process eliminates surds from the denominator and places them in the numerator, potentially changing their form in the process.

For example:

$${1\over{\sqrt{2}}}$$

This fraction needs simplifying, or rationalising. To do this we can use a trick, which cancels out the \(\sqrt{2}\) on the denominator.

If you haven't already spotted, we can multiply the entire fraction by \(\sqrt{2}\over{\sqrt{2}}\), since this is just the same as multiplying it by 1.

$${{1\over{\sqrt{2}}} \times {\sqrt{2}\over{\sqrt{2}}}}$$

The denominator of the new fraction will now just be 2 (since \({\sqrt{2}}\times {\sqrt{2}}=2\) )

Hence the new fraction will be:

$${\sqrt{2}\over{2}}$$

That is simply how you rationalise a denominator

BUT!(There's always a but.)

Not all denominators will be that easy to rationalise, unfortunately. Some will take a few extra steps.

Let's look at a fraction like this one:

$${1\over{1+\sqrt{2}}}$$

We can't use the method we just used, since a surd will still appear on the bottom, so we need to find another way to get rid of that \(\sqrt{2}\)

How about we try multiplying the denominator by (\(1-\sqrt{2}\))? Let's see what happens

$${{(1+\sqrt{2})}\times {(1-\sqrt{2})}=1^2+\sqrt{2}-\sqrt{2}-{(\sqrt{2})^2}}$$

You may have noticed that the \(\sqrt{2}\)s will cancel, and \((\sqrt{2})^2\) just equals 2, so we're left with (1-2), simply leaving us with a -1 on the bottom.

Remember we have to do the same to the numerator so we don't change the value of the surd:

$${{1\over{1+\sqrt{2}}} \times {1-\sqrt{2}\over{1-\sqrt{2}}}}$$

$${=}{{1-\sqrt{2}}\over{-1}}$$

$${=}{\sqrt{2}-1}$$

So with this example, not only has the denominator been rationalised, but the whole fraction has been simplified.

Let's try another example:

$${1\over{1-\sqrt{2}}}$$

This one has a minus on the bottom, and using the same logic as in the last example, we can multiply the numerator and denominator by \({(1+\sqrt{2})}\)

$${{1\over{1-\sqrt{2}}} \times {1+\sqrt{2}\over{1+\sqrt{2}}}}$$

$${=}{1+\sqrt{2}\over{1^2-\sqrt{2}+\sqrt{2}-{(\sqrt{2})^2}}}$$

$${=}{1+\sqrt{2}\over{-1}}$$

$${=}{-1-\sqrt{2}}$$

Exam Tip:

On certain calculators, inputting an "un-rationalised" fraction will output a rationalised and simplified version, which can be helpful for checking your answer. However, it also means that you should be very careful in showing every step of your working, or they might think you cheated!



Now that you’ve learned the concepts, let's test your understanding with a few questions.

Rationalise the following: $${2\over{\sqrt{3}}}$$

A. \( 3\sqrt{2} \over{3} \)
B. \( \sqrt{3}\over{2} \)
C. \(2\sqrt{3} \over{3} \)
D. \( 6\sqrt{2} \)

Given the expression: $$\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}$$

To rationalise the denominator, multiply the numerator and the denominator by \(\sqrt{3}\):

$$\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}}$$

The denominator becomes: $$\sqrt{3} \cdot \sqrt{3} = 3$$

The numerator becomes: $$2 \cdot \sqrt{3} = 2\sqrt{3}$$

Thus, the rationalised expression is: $$\frac{2\sqrt{3}}{3}$$

Rationalise and simplify the following: $${3\over{2+\sqrt{3}}}$$

A. \( 3(2-\sqrt{3}) \)
B. \( {2+\sqrt{3}}\over{3} \)
C. \(2\sqrt{3} + 3 \)
D. \( 3\sqrt{2} \)

Given the expression: $$\frac{3}{2 + \sqrt{3}}$$

To rationalise the denominator, multiply the numerator and the denominator by the conjugate of the denominator:

$$\frac{3}{2 + \sqrt{3}} \times \frac{2 - \sqrt{3}}{2 - \sqrt{3}}$$

The denominator becomes: $$(2 + \sqrt{3})(2 - \sqrt{3}) = 2^2 - (\sqrt{3})^2 = 4 - 3 = 1$$

The numerator becomes: $$3 \cdot (2 - \sqrt{3}) = 6 - 3\sqrt{3}$$

Thus, the simplified expression is: $$3(2 - \sqrt{3})$$





Indices

Now we have finished with surds, lets have a look at another GCSE topic in a bit more depth.

Indices are a core part of maths, especially in this A-level course. They appear in almost every module, so it is vital that you understand the basic rules and principles when dealing with powers.

For an expression in the form \(a^n\), we call the '\(a\)' term the base, and the '\(n\)' term the exponent or power.

Laws of Indices:

Product of powers with the same base:

When multiplying two numbers with the same base but different powers, you can add the powers and raise the base to this sum, like so:

\[a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}\]

Here is an example of this using numbers:

\[2^4 \times 2^3 = 2^7\]

Quotient of powers with the same base:

When dividing two numbers that share the same base but have different powers, you can simplify the expression by subtracting the powers and raising the base to the resulting power, like so:

\[{{a^m} \over {a^n}} = {a^{m-n}}\]

Here is an example of this using numbers:

\[{{3^6} \over {3^4}} = {3^{2}}=9\]

Powers of powers

Sometimes, you may encounter a number raised to a power, which is itself raised to another power. To simplify this, you multiply the exponents and use the product as the new exponent.

\[(a^m)^n=a^{mn}\]

Here is an example of this using numbers:

\[(4^2)^3=4^6\]

Powers of product

When you raise a product of two or more factors to a power, you can apply the power to each factor individually.

\[(ab)^n=a^nb^n\]

Here is an example of this using numbers:

\[(4x)^3=4^3x^3=64x^3\]

Powers of quotient

When you raise a quotient of two or more factors to a power, you can apply the power to each factor individually.

\[ \left( \frac{a}{b} \right)^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n} \]

Here is an example of this using numbers:

\[\left({3\over{4}}\right)^2={3^2\over{4^2}}=9/16\]

Zero indices

Raising any number to the power of zero results in a value of one.

\[a^0=1\]

Here are some examples of this using numbers:

\[999999999^0=1\]

\[-0.0006^0=1\]

Negative indices

Raising any number to a negative power will result in the reciprocal of the base raised to the corresponding positive power.

Note: A reciprocal is the inverse of a number. To find the reciprocal of a number, you divide 1 by that number. For example , the reciprocal of 2 would be \(\frac{1}{2}\), and the reciprocal of \(\frac{2}{3}\) would be \(\frac{3}{2}\) etc.

\[a^{-n}={\left({1\over{a}}\right)^n}\]

Here are some examples of this using numbers:

\[2^{-3}={\left({1\over{2}}\right)^3}={1\over{8}}\]

\[{\left({2\over{3}}\right)^{-4}}={\left({3\over{2}}\right)^4}={\frac{81}{16}}\]

Fractional indices

Raising any number to a fractional power involves raising the number to the power of the numerator and then taking the root indicated by the denominator.

\[\large {a^{\LARGE{\frac{m}{n}}}= \ \sqrt[n]{a^m}}\]

Note: This should not be confused with \(n \times \sqrt{a^m}\). It is the nth root of \(\sqrt{a^m}\). Additionally, this process can be done in any order. For example, if it's easier to take the nth root first and then raise it to the power of the numerator, you can do it in that sequence.

Here is an example of this using numbers:

\[\large 8^{\frac{4}{3}}=\sqrt[3]{8^4}= \left(\sqrt[3]{8}\right)^4 = 2^4 = 16\]

In this example, it is easier to take the cube root of 8 before applying the 4th power, as the cube root of 8 is 2.


These are the fundamental rules for indices. It is important to thoroughly review and understand them, as they will frequently appear in most A-level Maths modules. Now, let's proceed to some equations involving indices.

Equations involving indices:

You can apply the rules you have just learned to different types of equations. For example, consider the following equation:

\[2^{3n}=2^{12}\]

Since the bases are the same, the powers must be equal. Therefore, we have:

\[3n=12\] \[{n=4}\]

You may also see equations like this:

\[{2^6 \times {2^{2x}}}={2^{10}}\]

Using the first rule we learnt, we can deduce the following:

\[6+2x=10\]\[x=2\]

Another type of equation you might come across is this:

\[5^{2x+1}=125\]

You may recognise that \(125 = 5^3\), so

\[5^{2x+1}=5^3\]\[{2x+1}=3\]\[x=1\]

You might also see equations like:

\[x^{\frac{3}{2}} = 8\]

To solve this, you need to get rid of the exponent on the left side. You can do this by raising both sides of the equation to the power of \(\frac{2}{3}\). This will make the exponent on \( x \) equal to 1. Just remember to do the same to both sides of the equation.

\[ \left( x^{\frac{3}{2}} \right)^{\frac{2}{3}} = 8^{\frac{2}{3}} \]

This simplifies to:

\[ x = \left( 8^{\frac{2}{3}} \right) \]

Since the cube root of 8 is 2, and \(2^2 = 4\), we have:

\[ x = 4 \]

Combining Indices and Surds

Now we've covered the rules we can apply to surds and indices, it's time to look at some questions that combine the two.

In an exam, it is common to see an expression that contains both surds and indices, and you will be asked to simplify the expression.

Let's look at an example:

Fully simplify \[\frac{10 +5x -2x^{\frac{1}{2}}-x^\frac{3}{2}}{5-\sqrt{x}}\] [4 Marks]

This might look quite daunting at first, but we can break it down into small steps to simplify the expression, using the rules we've looked at.

Firstly, let's rationalise the denominator.

\[{\frac{10 +5x -2x^{\frac{1}{2}}-x^\frac{3}{2}}{5-\sqrt{x}}} \times {\frac{5+\sqrt{x}}{5+\sqrt{x}}}\]= \[{\frac{(10 +5x -2x^{\frac{1}{2}}-x^\frac{3}{2})(5+\sqrt{x})}{(5-\sqrt{x})(5+\sqrt{x})}}\]= \[{\frac{(10 +5x -2x^{\frac{1}{2}}-x^\frac{3}{2})(5+\sqrt{x})}{25-x}}\]

Now we have to handle the numerator. To expand these brackets, we should start by converting \(\sqrt{x}\) to \(x^{\frac{1}{2}}\), to ensure every term is in the same form. \[{\frac{(10 +5x -2x^{\frac{1}{2}}-x^\frac{3}{2})(5+x^{\frac{1}{2}})}{25-x}}\]

Now we can expand the brackets and simplify the numerator.

First, multiply each term in the first bracket by \(5\):

\[ (10 \cdot 5) + (5x \cdot 5) - (2x^{\frac{1}{2}} \cdot 5) - (x^{\frac{3}{2}} \cdot 5) \]

\[ =50 + 25x - 10x^{\frac{1}{2}} - 5x^{\frac{3}{2}} \]

Next, multiply each term in the first bracket by \(x^{\frac{1}{2}}\):

Note: Recall that you can add the powers when multiplying with the same base. An \(x\) with no power is just \(x^1\).

\[ (10 \cdot x^{\frac{1}{2}}) + (5x \cdot x^{\frac{1}{2}}) - (2x^{\frac{1}{2}} \cdot x^{\frac{1}{2}}) - (x^{\frac{3}{2}} \cdot x^{\frac{1}{2}}) \]

\[ =10x^{\frac{1}{2}} + 5x^{\frac{3}{2}} - 2x - x^2 \]

Combine these results:

\( 50 + 25x - 10x^{\frac{1}{2}} - 5x^{\frac{3}{2}} \) + \(10x^{\frac{1}{2}} + 5x^{\frac{3}{2}} - 2x - x^2 \)

= \[ 50 + (25x - 2x) + (-10x^{\frac{1}{2}} + 10x^{\frac{1}{2}})\] + \[(-5x^{\frac{3}{2}} + 5x^{\frac{3}{2}}) - x^2\] = \[ 50 + 23x - x^2 \]

After expanding and combining like terms, the simplified expression is:

\[ \frac{50 + 23x - x^2}{25 - x} \]

Although it is not immediately obvious, you can actually simplify this further by factorising the numerator. Since we haven't covered quadratics in the course yet, don't worry too much if you do not understand this next step, but you may be able to understand it using your knowledge from GCSE maths.

\({50 + 23x - x^2}\) can be factorised to \((25-x)(2+x)\), so the expression can be re-writtten as \[\frac{(25-x)(2+x)}{25 - x}\]

The \((25-x)\) on the numerator and the denominator will cancel out, leaving us with simply \[x+2\]

And that is the fully simplified version of the original expression!

That was quite a difficult example, so if you followed the whole way through, well done. It came directly from a past AQA exam paper.

Topic Review: Surds and Indices

In this topic, you have learned the following key concepts:

  • Definition of a Surd: A surd is an irrational number that can be expressed as a root of a rational number. Common examples include \(\sqrt{2}\) and \(\sqrt[3]{5}\).
  • Arithmetic Functions of Surds: Includes addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division of surds. For example, \(\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{2} = 2\sqrt{2}\) and \(\sqrt{3} \times \sqrt{5} = \sqrt{15}\).
  • Simplifying Surds: Reducing surds to their simplest form. For instance, \(\sqrt{50}\) simplifies to \(5\sqrt{2}\).
  • Expanding Brackets with Surds: Applying the distributive property to expressions involving surds. For example, \((\sqrt{2} + 3)(\sqrt{2} - 1)\) simplifies to \(2 - \sqrt{2} + 3\sqrt{2} - 3 = -1 + 2\sqrt{2}\).
  • Rationalising the Denominator: Making the denominator of a fraction rational by multiplying by a suitable expression. For instance, \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\) is rationalised to \(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\).
  • Laws of Indices: Rules for manipulating expressions with indices, such as \((a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n})\), \(\left(a^m\right)^n = a^{mn}\), and \(\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}\).
  • Solving Equations Involving Indices: Applying the laws of indices to solve equations. For example, solving \(2^x = 32\) involves recognizing that \(32 = 2^5\), so \(x = 5\).
  • Combining Surds and Indices: Simplifying complex expressions that involve both surds and indices, such as \(\sqrt{2^4} = \sqrt{16} = 4\).
