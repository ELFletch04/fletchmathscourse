Quadratics



An Introduction to Quadratics:

A quadratic equation is a type of polynomial equation that involves the square of the variable. It has the general form: \[ax^2+bx+c=0\] where \(a\),\(b\) and \(c\) are constants, and \(a\neq 0\)

A quadratic equation typically produces a curve that appears as a parabola on a graph, like so:

Here is an example of a quadratic equation using numbers:\[2x^2+3x+1=0\]

Solving Quadratics

There are 4 approaches you can take to solve a quadratic equation:

  1. Factorising
  2. Completing the square
  3. The quadratic formula
  4. Scientific calculator

    5. There are pros and cons of using each method, and certain methods are better than others depending on the nature of the question.

    Let's go through each of the methods.

    1. Factorising

    Factorising a quadratic equation involves rewriting it from the standard form \[ax^2 + bx + c = 0\] into its factorised form \[(px + m)(qx + n) = 0\] where \(p\), \(q\), \(m\), and \(n\) are constants.

    Once the quadratic equation is expressed in this factorised form, you can set each bracket equal to zero and solve for \(x\) to find the solutions.

    It is most commonly used when trying to find the *roots* of an equation.

    Remember, this method is a key technique you should be familiar with from your GCSE studies. Feel free to skip this section if you are already confident in factorising quadratics.


    Let's go through a worked example:

    Question

    Factorise and solve this quadratic equation\[x^2+5x+6=0\]

    Solution

    To factorise \(x^2+5x+6\), we need to express it in the form \((px+m)(qx+n)\).

    We are looking for two numbers that:

    • Multiply to the constant term \(6\)
    • Add up to the coefficient of \(x\), which is \(5\)

    Note: This method only works when the coefficient of \(x^2\) is \(1\).

    The numbers 2 and 3 fit this criteria, since

    • \(2 \times{3}=6\)
    • \(2 + 3 = 5\)

    So, we can factorise \(x^2+5x+6\) as \[(x+2)(x+3)\] Hence, \[(x+2)(x+3)=0\]

    Note: In order to check this, you can expand it back out to get the original equation.

    Now we need to solve it for x. To do this, set each bracket equal to 0.

    • \((x+2)=0\)

    \(\Rightarrow x=-2\)

    • \((x+3)=0\)

    \(\Rightarrow x=-3\)

    Hence the solutions to \(x^2+5x+6\) are:

    \(x=-2\)

    OR

    \(x=-3\)

    Let's try another example:

    Question

    Factorise and solve the following quadratic equation:\[2x^2+7x+3=0\]

    Solution

    For quadratic equations where the coefficient of \(x^2\) is not equal to 1, the usual method of finding two numbers that add up to the middle coefficient and multiply to the constant term may not be directly applicable.

    Nevertheless, with some trial and error, it is often straightforward to factorise and solve such equations. Here is how you can approach it:


    When factorising the expression, you know that the coefficients of \(x\) in both brackets must multiply to equal 2, since this is the coefficient of \(x^2\) in the equation.

    We can use this knowledge to set up our brackets.

    \[(2x+a)(x+b)\]

    Now we just need to find the values of \(a\) and \(b\)

    It is clear that \(a \times b = 3\), just by looking at the original equation, as they are the only values with no \(x\)`s in.

    So we can assume \(a\) and \(b\) take values of 1 and 3, but we need to test which one is which.


    Let's try \(a=3\) and \(b=1\)

    \[(2x+3)(x+1)\]

    If you expand this, you will notice you do not get the original equation, you get \(2x^2+5x+3\), so we know it must be the other way around.

    Let's now try \(a=1\) and \(b=3\)

    \[(2x+1)(x+3)\]

    When you expand this, you get \(2x^2+x+6x+3\), and upon collecting your like terms, you will get the original equation \(2x^2+7x+3=0\), meaning it has been factorised successfully!

    Our factorised equation is: \[(2x+1)(x+3)=0\]

    The solving step is exactly the same as before; simply set each bracket equal to 0.

    Here \(x=-\frac{1}{2}\) or \(x=-3\)

    2. Completing the square

    Completing the square is a method of solving quadratics that is most commonly used when trying to find the vertex or *turning point* of the curve.

    Some people tend to prefer this method to solve quadratics, as it can be more efficient for solving certain types of equations.

    Let's go through a few worked examples:

    Question

    Solve this equation by completing the square: \[x^2+5x+6=0\]

    Solution

    We are aiming to get this in the form \((ax+b)^2+c=0\)

    The coefficient \(a\) must be 1, since \(1^2\) is \(1\). The \(b\) will be half of the coefficient of \(x\). This method works because, when expanded, the squared term produces twice the middle term. In this case, the coefficient of \(x\) is 5, so the \(b\) will be \(\frac{5}{2}\).

    So our bracket will be \((x+{\frac{5}{2}})^2\)

    To find c, we already have a \(+6\) from the original equation. However, if we were to expand our new bracket, it would be equivalent to \(x^2+5x+\frac{25}{4}\)

    We don't want that \(\frac{25}{4}\), so we take it away.

    Note: For a nice little trick, just remember to always take away the square of the constant in the bracket.

    That means we are left with \[\left(x+{\frac{5}{2}}\right)^2+6-{\frac{25}{4}}=0\]

    Which simplifies to \[\left(x+{\frac{5}{2}}\right)^2-{\frac{1}{4}}=0\]

    Now, to solve this equation we can simply just do some rearanging.

    \[\left(x+{\frac{5}{2}}\right)^2={\frac{1}{4}}\]

    \[\sqrt{\left(x+{\frac{5}{2}}\right)^2}={\sqrt{\frac{1}{4}}}\]

    Don't forget the two possible solutions to \(\sqrt{\frac{1}{4}}\)

    \[x+{\frac{5}{2}}={\pm\frac{1}{2}}\]

    Hence our two solutions are \[x=-{\frac{5}{2}}+\frac{1}{2}\]\[x=-{\frac{5}{2}}-\frac{1}{2}\]

    \(\Longrightarrow\) \[x=-2\]\[x=-3\]
