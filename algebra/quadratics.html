Quadratics

An Introduction to Quadratics

A quadratic equation is a polynomial equation of the second degree, having the general form:

\[ax^2 + bx + c = 0\] where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are constants, and \(a \neq 0\).

Graphically, a quadratic equation produces a parabola. Here is an example:

For instance, the quadratic equation \[2x^2 + 3x + 1 = 0\] can be graphed as shown.

Solving Quadratic Equations

There are four main methods for solving quadratic equations:

  • Factorising
  • Completing the square
  • The quadratic formula
  • Scientific calculator

Each method has its advantages and is suited to different types of quadratic equations. We will explore each method, but before doing so let's see how we can speed up the process, by determining the number of roots (or solutions) an equation has before attampting to solve it, using something called the discriminant.

Discriminant:

The discriminant is part of the quadratic formula and provides insight into the roots of the quadratic equation:

  • Two Distinct Real Roots: If \( b^2 - 4ac > 0 \), the quadratic equation has two distinct real roots.
  • One Repeated Real Root: If \( b^2 - 4ac = 0 \), the quadratic equation has one repeated real root.
  • No Real Roots (Complex Roots): If \( b^2 - 4ac < 0 \), the quadratic equation has no real roots.

Let's quickly test your understanding of the discrimintant.

Example:

How many solutions does the following equation have?: \[x^2+5x+6\]

A. \( 0 \)
B. \( 1 \)
C. \( 2 \)
D. Not enough information

1. Factorising

Factorising involves rewriting the quadratic equation from the form \[ax^2 + bx + c = 0\] into \[(px + m)(qx + n) = 0\], where \(p\), \(q\), \(m\), and \(n\) are constants.

Once in factorised form, set each bracket equal to zero and solve for \(x\). This method is useful for finding the roots of the equation.

Example:

Question: Factorise and solve \[x^2 + 5x + 6 = 0\]

Solution:

To factorise \[x^2 + 5x + 6\], find two numbers that multiply to \(6\) and add up to \(5\). These numbers are \(2\) and \(3\), so:

\[x^2 + 5x + 6 = (x + 2)(x + 3)\]

Setting each bracket to zero gives:

\[x + 2 = 0 \implies x = -2\]

\[x + 3 = 0 \implies x = -3\]

The solutions are \(x = -2\) and \(x = -3\).

Another Example: Factorise and solve \[2x^2 + 7x + 3 = 0\]

For equations where \(a \neq 1\), trial and error may help:

Assume the factorised form is \[(2x + a)(x + b)\]. We need \(a \times b = 3\) and coefficients to match. Trying \(a = 1\) and \(b = 3\) or \(a = 3\) and \(b = 1\), we find:

\[2x^2 + 7x + 3 = (2x + 1)(x + 3)\]

Solving this gives:

\[2x + 1 = 0 \implies x = -\frac{1}{2}\]

\[x + 3 = 0 \implies x = -3\]

The solutions are \(x = -\frac{1}{2}\) and \(x = -3\).

2. Completing the Square

Completing the square is a method used to solve quadratic equations and to find the vertex of a parabola. Here’s how you can complete the square using the method where you halve the coefficient of \(x\) and subtract its square:

  1. Start with the standard form: Begin with the equation \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\). If \(a \neq 1\), divide through by \(a\) to make the coefficient of \(x^2\) equal to 1.
  2. Rearrange: Move the constant term \(c\) to the other side of the equation.
  3. Halve the coefficient of \(x\): Take \(\frac{b}{2}\), square it, and add and subtract this value on the left-hand side of the equation.
  4. Rewrite and solve: Rearrange and solve for \(x\).

Example 1: Solve \[x^2 + 6x + 5 = 0\] by completing the square.

Solution:

1. Start with the equation:

\[x^2 + 6x + 5 = 0\]

2. Move the constant term to the other side:

\[x^2 + 6x = -5\]

3. Halve the coefficient of \(x\), which is 6. Half of 6 is 3. This value goes into the bracket as \(x + 3\). Square this number to get 9. To complete the square, subtract this squared value (9) on the left-hand side of the equation and adjust the constant term on the right-hand side. This transforms the equation into the following form:

\[(x + 3)^2 - 9 = -5\]

What we've done is transform the equation into a form that is much easier to manipulate and solve. The trick of halving the coefficient of \(x\) and subtracting its square will work every time, provided the coefficient of \(x^2\) in the original equation is 1.

4. Simplify the equation by moving -9 to the right side:

\[(x + 3)^2 = 4\]

5. Solve for \(x\) by taking the square root of both sides:

\[x + 3 = \pm \sqrt{4}\]

\[x + 3 = \pm 2\]

6. Isolate \(x\) to find the solutions:

\[x = -3 + 2 = -1\]

\[x = -3 - 2 = -5\]

The solutions are \(x = -1\) and \(x = -5\).

Example 2: Solve \[x^2 - 4x - 5 = 0\] by completing the square.

Solution:

1. Start with the equation:

\[x^2 - 4x - 5 = 0\]

2. Move the constant term to the other side:

\[x^2 - 4x = 5\]

3. Halve the coefficient of \(x\), which is -4. Half of -4 is -2. This value goes into the bracket as \(x - 2\). Square this number to get 4. To complete the square, subtract this squared value (4) on the left-hand side of the equation and adjust the constant term on the right-hand side. This transforms the equation into the following form:

\[(x - 2)^2 - 4 = 5\]

What we've done is transform the equation into a form that is much easier to manipulate and solve. The trick of halving the coefficient of \(x\) and subtracting its square will work every time, provided the coefficient of \(x^2\) in the original equation is 1.

4. Simplify the equation by moving -4 to the right side:

\[(x - 2)^2 = 9\]

5. Solve for \(x\) by taking the square root of both sides:

\[x - 2 = \pm \sqrt{9}\]

\[x - 2 = \pm 3\]

6. Isolate \(x\) to find the solutions:

\[x = 2 + 3 = 5\]

\[x = 2 - 3 = -1\]

The solutions are \(x = 5\) and \(x = -1\).

Have a go at this one:

Solve by completing the square:\[x^2+8x+7=0\]

A. \( x=-1 \) , \(x=7\)
B. \( x=-1/7 \) , \(x=1\)
C. \( x=1 \) , \(x=7\)
D. \( x=-1\) , \(x=-7\)

Correct! To solve the quadratic equation \( x^2 + 8x + 7 = 0 \) by completing the square, follow these steps:

1. Start with the original equation:

\[ x^2 + 8x + 7 = 0 \]

2. Complete the square on the left-hand side. To do this, we need to subtract the square of half the coefficient of \( x \). The coefficient of \( x \) is 8, so half of this value is 4, and squaring it gives 16.

\[ (x + 4)^2 - 16 + 7 = 0 \] \[ (x + 4)^2 - 9 = 0 \]

3. Take the constant term to the other side.

\[(x + 4)^2 = 9 \]

4. Take the square root of both sides:

\[x + 4 = \pm \sqrt{9} \] \[x + 4 = \pm 3\]

5. Solve for \( x \) by isolating it:

\[x = -4 \pm 3\]

This gives two solutions:

\[ x = -1 \quad \text{and} \quad x = -7\]

Thus, the solutions to the equation are \( x = -1 \) and \( x = -7 \).

Example 3: Solve \[2x^2 + 5x - 3 = 0\] by completing the square.

Solution:

1. Start with the equation:

\[2x^2 + 5x - 3 = 0\]

2. Divide the entire equation by 2 to make the coefficient of \(x^2\) equal to 1:

\[x^2 + \frac{5}{2}x - \frac{3}{2} = 0\]

3. Move the constant term to the other side:

\[x^2 + \frac{5}{2}x = \frac{3}{2}\]

4. Halve the coefficient of \(x\), which is \(\frac{5}{2}\). Half of \(\frac{5}{2}\) is \(\frac{5}{4}\). This value goes into the bracket as \(x + \frac{5}{4}\). Square this number to get \(\left(\frac{5}{4}\right)^2 = \frac{25}{16}\). To complete the square, subtract this squared value (\(\frac{25}{16}\)) on the left-hand side of the equation and adjust the constant term on the right-hand side. This transforms the equation into the following form:

\[\left(x + \frac{5}{4}\right)^2 - \frac{25}{16} = \frac{3}{2}\]

5. Simplify the equation by moving \(-\frac{25}{16}\) to the right side. Convert \(\frac{3}{2}\) to a fraction with the same denominator (\(\frac{24}{16}\)):

\[\left(x + \frac{5}{4}\right)^2 = \frac{24}{16} + \frac{25}{16}\]

\[\left(x + \frac{5}{4}\right)^2 = \frac{49}{16}\]

6. Solve for \(x\) by taking the square root of both sides:

\[x + \frac{5}{4} = \pm \sqrt{\frac{49}{16}}\]

\[x + \frac{5}{4} = \pm \frac{7}{4}\]

7. Isolate \(x\) to find the solutions:

\[x = -\frac{5}{4} + \frac{7}{4} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}\]

\[x = -\frac{5}{4} - \frac{7}{4} = -\frac{12}{4} = -3\]

The solutions are \(x = \frac{1}{2}\) and \(x = -3\).


Try this more difficult example:

Solve by completing the square:\[x^2+8x+7=0\]

A. \( x = \frac{-3 + \sqrt{41}}{8} \) , \( x = \frac{-3 - \sqrt{41}}{8} \)
B. \( x = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{5}}{4} \) , \( x = \frac{-1 - \sqrt{5}}{4} \)
C. \( x = \frac{-2 + \sqrt{10}}{5} \) , \( x = \frac{-2 - \sqrt{10}}{5} \)
D. \( x = \frac{-3 + \sqrt{41}}{8} \) , \( x = \frac{-1 - \sqrt{41}}{8} \)

1. Start with the original equation:

\[ 4x^2 + 3x - 2 = 0 \]

2. Factor out 4 from the first two terms:

\[ 4 \left(x^2 + \frac{3}{4}x\right) - 2 = 0 \]

3. Complete the square inside the brackets. Subtract the square of half the coefficient of \(x\). The coefficient of \(x\) inside the brackets is \(\frac{3}{4}\). Half of this value is \(\frac{3}{8}\), and squaring it gives \(\frac{9}{64}\):

\[ 4 \left[\left(x + \frac{3}{8}\right)^2 - \frac{9}{64}\right] - 2 = 0 \]

4. Expand the bracket by multiplying both terms by 4:

\[ 4 \left(x + \frac{3}{8}\right)^2 - 4 \cdot \frac{9}{64} - 2 = 0 \]

5. Simplifying this gives:

\[ 4 \left(x + \frac{3}{8}\right)^2 - \frac{41}{16} = 0 \]

6. Add \(\frac{41}{16}\) to both sides:

\[ 4 \left(x + \frac{3}{8}\right)^2 = \frac{41}{16} \]

7. Divide both sides by 4 to simplify:

\[ \left(x + \frac{3}{8}\right)^2 = \frac{41}{64} \]

8. Solve for \(x\) by taking the square root of both sides:

\[ x + \frac{3}{8} = \pm \frac{\sqrt{41}}{8} \]

9. Isolate \(x\) to find the solutions:

\[ x = -\frac{3}{8} \pm \frac{\sqrt{41}}{8} \] \[ x = \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{41}}{8} \]

Hence, the solutions to the equation are:

\[ x = \frac{-3 + \sqrt{41}}{8} \quad \text{and} \quad x = \frac{-3 - \sqrt{41}}{8} \]

3. The Quadratic Formula

The quadratic formula is used to solve any quadratic equation and is given by:

\[x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\]

Example:

Question: Solve \[2x^2 + 3x + 1 = 0\] using the quadratic formula.

Solution:

Here, \(a = 2\), \(b = 3\), and \(c = 1\). Plug these values into the formula:

\[x = \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{3^2 - 4 \cdot 2 \cdot 1}}{2 \cdot 2}\]

\[x = \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{9 - 8}}{4}\]

\[x = \frac{-3 \pm 1}{4}\]

The solutions are:

\[x = \frac{-3 + 1}{4} = -\frac{1}{2}\]

\[x = \frac{-3 - 1}{4} = -1\]


Solve by using the quadratic formula:\[ 4x^2 + 2x - 1 = 0 \]

A. \( x = \frac{-2 + \sqrt{13}}{6} \) , \( x = \frac{-2 - \sqrt{13}}{6} \)
B. \( x = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{5}}{4} \) , \( x = \frac{-1 - \sqrt{5}}{4} \)
C. \( x = \frac{-3 + \sqrt{17}}{6} \) , \( x = \frac{-3 - \sqrt{17}}{6} \)
D. \( x = \frac{-4 + \sqrt{21}}{5} \) , \( x = \frac{-4 - \sqrt{21}}{5} \)
To solve the quadratic equation \( 4x^2 + 2x - 1 = 0 \) using the quadratic formula, follow these steps:
  1. Identify the coefficients \( a \), \( b \), and \( c \) from the equation \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \). For our equation \( 4x^2 + 2x - 1 = 0 \), we have:
    • \( a = 4 \)
    • \( b = 2 \)
    • \( c = -1 \)
  2. Use the quadratic formula: \[ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \]
  3. Plug in the values of \( a \), \( b \), and \( c \): \[ x = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{2^2 - 4 \cdot 4 \cdot (-1)}}{2 \cdot 4} \]
  4. Calculate the discriminant: \[ \sqrt{2^2 - 4 \cdot 4 \cdot (-1)} = \sqrt{4 + 16} = \sqrt{20} = 2\sqrt{5} \]
  5. Substitute the discriminant back into the formula: \[ x = \frac{-2 \pm 2\sqrt{5}}{8} \]
  6. Simplify the expression: \[ x = \frac{-2 + 2\sqrt{5}}{8} = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{5}}{4} \quad \text{and} \quad x = \frac{-2 - 2\sqrt{5}}{8} = \frac{-1 - \sqrt{5}}{4} \]
Thus, the correct answers are: \[ x = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{5}}{4} \quad \text{and} \quad x = \frac{-1 - \sqrt{5}}{4} \]

4. Using a Scientific Calculator

Scientific calculators often feature a built-in quadratic solver. This function allows you to input the values for \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) from the quadratic equation \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\) to quickly find the roots.

While this method is fast and convenient, it may not provide much insight into the underlying techniques of solving quadratics. For a deeper understanding, it’s beneficial to practice solving equations manually using other methods.

For detailed instructions on how to use the quadratic solver on your specific calculator model, refer to online guides or the user manual for your device.

Exam Tip

Before relying on your calculator, try to solve the quadratic equation manually using one of the other methods. This will help reinforce your understanding and ensure accuracy. If you’re short on time during an exam, using the calculator can be a valuable tool. Just be sure to double-check that you’ve entered the coefficients correctly to avoid any errors.


Graphing Quadratics



A quadratic equation represents a parabola when graphed. Understanding how to graph a quadratic equation involves identifying key features: the vertex, roots (x-intercepts), y-intercept, and the shape of the curve. Below, we'll walk through each of these features and use Desmos for visual demonstrations.

1. Vertex

Finding the Vertex Using Completed Square Form



Once you have rewritten a quadratic function in the completed square form, identifying the vertex is straightforward. The completed square form is:

\[ y = a(x - h)^2 + k \]

Understanding the Vertex Form

In the completed square form:

  • \( (h, k) \) represents the vertex of the quadratic function.
  • \( h \) is the \( x \)-coordinate of the vertex, and it is found inside the brackets. If the form is \( (x - h) \), then \( h \) is the opposite sign of what appears in the brackets.
  • \( k \) is the \( y \)-coordinate of the vertex and is the constant term outside the squared term.

Example

Consider the quadratic function:

\[ y = 2(x + 3)^2 - 7 \]

This function is already in the completed square form:

\[ y = a(x - h)^2 + k \]

Here, \( a = 2 \), \( h = -3 \), and \( k = -7 \). Thus:

  • The vertex \( (h, k) \) is \( (-3, -7) \).

So, the vertex of the quadratic function \( y = 2(x + 3)^2 - 7 \) is \( (-3, -7) \).

2. Roots (x-intercepts)

The roots of a quadratic equation are the x-values where the graph crosses the x-axis:

You can find them by solving the equation for \(x\), which you have just learned how to do.

Here are the three possible cases showing how the number of roots affects where the graph crosses the x-axis:

Two Distinct Roots:

The graph intersects the x-axis at two points.

Repeated Root:

The graph touches the x-axis at one point.

No Real Roots:

The graph does not intersect the x-axis.

For example, consider the quadratic equation \( y = x^2 - 4x + 4 \). We can solve it by factorising:

\( x^2 - 4x + 4 = (x - 2)(x - 2) = 0 \)

This means we have a repeated root, where \(x=2\)

Hence, the graph only touches the x-axis at \(x=2\).

3. Y-Intercept

The y-intercept is the point where the graph crosses the y-axis. It is found by setting \(x = 0\) in the equation \(ax^2 + bx +\) \(c\). The y-intercept is simply the constant term \(c\) in the equation.

For example, consider the quadratic equation \(y = x^2 - 4x + 3\). When \(x = 0\), the value of \(y\) is:

\(y = (0)^2 - 4(0) + 3 = 3\)

So, the y-intercept is at \(y = 3\), which is the constant term \(c\) in the equation.

4. Shape of the Curve

The shape of the parabola depends on the coefficient \(a\) in the quadratic equation \(ax^2 + bx + c\). If \(a\) is positive, the parabola is a "U" shape, like it's going up; if \(a\) is negative, it is more sort of an "n" shape, like it's going down. The magnitude of \(a\) affects how "steep" the curve is.

Visualize the effect of different values of \(a\) on the shape of the curve:

Positive x:

\(y = x^2 - 2x + 1\)

Negative x:

\(y = -x^2 + 2x - 1\)

Example: Graphing a Quadratic Equation

Let's graph the quadratic equation:

\[ y = 2x^2 - 4x - 6 \]

This quadratic has a vertex, roots, and y-intercept that we can identify:

  1. Vertex: To find the vertex, complete the square:
    • Start with the equation: \( y = 2x^2 - 4x - 6 \)
    • Factor out the 2 from the \(x\)-terms (to make the coefficient of \(x= 1\)):

      \( y = 2(x^2 - 2x) - 6 \)

    • Complete the square inside the brackets: \[ y = 2 \left((x - 1)^2 - 1 \right) - 6 \]
    • Expand and simplify: \[ y = 2(x - 1)^2 - 8 \]
    • The vertex is at \( (1, -8) \).
  2. Roots: Find where the parabola intersects the x-axis by solving \(2x^2 - 4x - 6 = 0\). This requires using the quadratic formula or factoring.
  3. Y-Intercept: We can see that the constant term in this equation is \(-6\), hence the y-intercept is \((0,-6)\).

Topic Review: Quadratics

In this topic, you have learned the following key concepts:

  • Definition of a Quadratic Equation: A quadratic equation is any equation that can be written in the form \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\), where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are constants and \(a \ne 0\). The graph of a quadratic equation is a parabola.
  • Factorising Quadratics: To solve quadratic equations, you can often factorize them into the form \((x - p)(x - q) = 0\) where \(p\) and \(q\) are the roots of the equation. This involves finding two numbers that multiply to \(ac\) (the product of \(a\) and \(c\)) and add to \(b\).
  • Completing the Square: This method involves rewriting the quadratic equation in the form \((x - h)^2 = k\), where \((h, k)\) represents the vertex of the parabola. This can be useful for solving quadratic equations and for graphing them.
  • Quadratic Formula: The roots of a quadratic equation \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\) can be found using the quadratic formula: \[ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \] This formula provides the solutions directly and can be used when factorizing is difficult.
  • Discriminant: The discriminant of a quadratic equation is given by \(\Delta = b^2 - 4ac\). It determines the nature of the roots:
    • If \(\Delta > 0\), there are two distinct real roots.
    • If \(\Delta = 0\), there is exactly one real root (a repeated root).
    • If \(\Delta < 0\), there are no real roots (the roots are complex).
  • Graphing Quadratic Functions: The graph of a quadratic function \(y = ax^2 + bx + c\) is a parabola. The vertex form \((x - h)^2 = k\) helps in identifying the vertex of the parabola, and the axis of symmetry is given by \(x = -\frac{b}{2a}\).
