Simultaneous Equations


Introduction to Simultaneous Equations

Simultaneous equations consist of a set of equations involving multiple variables which are solved together. The solutions must satisfy all the given equations simultaneously. This topic serves as a refresher from GCSE Maths, revisiting the fundamental concepts and methods used to solve such systems.

Linear Simultaneous Equations

Linear simultaneous equations are those where each equation is of the form \(ax + by = c\). Common methods for solving these include substitution and elimination.

Substitution Method:

In this method, you solve one of the equations for one variable and then substitute that expression into the other equation.

Example: Substitution

Solve the following system of equations:

\[ \begin{align*} x + y &= 5 \\ x - y &= 1 \end{align*} \]

**Solution:**

  1. Rearrange the first equation to express \(x\) in terms of \(y\):

    2. \[ x = 5 - y \]

  2. Substitute \(x = 5 - y\) into the second equation:

    3. \[ (5 - y) - y = 1 \implies 5 - 2y = 1 \implies -2y = 1 - 5 \implies -2y = -4 \implies y = 2 \]

  3. Substitute \(y = 2\) back into the first equation:

    4. \[ x + 2 = 5 \implies x = 5 - 2 \implies x = 3 \]

  4. **Solution:** \(x = 3\), \(y = 2\)

Try this example:

Solve the following system of equations using the substitution method:

\[ \begin{align*} x + 2y &= 7 \\ 3x - y &= 5 \end{align*} \]

A. \((x, y) = (-\frac{17}{7},\frac{16}{7} )\)
B. \((x, y) = (-\frac{17}{7},-\frac{16}{7} )\)
C. \((x, y) = (\frac{17}{7},\frac{16}{7} )\)
D. \((x, y) = (3, 4)\)

**Solution:**

  1. Rearrange the first equation to express \(x\) in terms of \(y\):

    2. \[ x = 7 - 2y \]

  2. Substitute \(x = 7 - 2y\) into the second equation:

    3. \[ 3(7 - 2y) - y = 5 \implies 21 - 6y - y = 5 \implies -7y = 5 - 21 \implies -7y = -16 \implies y = \frac{16}{7} \]

  3. Substitute \(y = \frac{16}{7}\) back into \(x = 7 - 2y\):

    4. \[ x = 7 - 2 \left(\frac{16}{7}\right) \implies x = 7 - \frac{32}{7} \implies x = \frac{49 - 32}{7} = \frac{17}{7} \]

  4. **Solution:** \(x = \frac{17}{7}\), \(y = \frac{16}{7}\)

Elimination Method:

This method involves adding or subtracting equations to eliminate one of the variables, making it easier to solve for the remaining variable.

Example: Elimination

Solve the following system of equations:

\[ \begin{align*} 2x + 3y &= 7 \\ 4x - y &= 5 \end{align*} \]

**Solution:**

  1. Multiply the second equation by 3 to align the coefficients of \(y\):

    2. \[ \begin{align*} 2x + 3y &= 7 \\ 12x - 3y &= 15 \end{align*} \]

  2. Add the equations to eliminate \(y\):

    3. \[ (2x + 3y) + (12x - 3y) = 7 + 15 \implies 14x = 22 \implies x = \frac{22}{14} = \frac{11}{7} \]

  3. Substitute \(x = \frac{11}{7}\) into the first equation:

    4. \[ 2\left(\frac{11}{7}\right) + 3y = 7 \implies \frac{22}{7} + 3y = 7 \implies 3y = 7 - \frac{22}{7} = \frac{49 - 22}{7} = \frac{27}{7} \implies y = \frac{27}{21} = \frac{9}{7} \]

  4. **Solution:** \(x = \frac{11}{7}\), \(y = \frac{9}{7}\)

Solve the following system of equations using the elimination method:

\[ \begin{align*} 4x - 2y &= 8 \\ 2x + 3y &= 7 \end{align*} \]

A. \( (x,y) = (-\frac{3}{4},\frac{19}{8} ) \)
B. \( (x,y) = (-\frac{3}{4},\frac{18}{9} ) \)
C. \( (x,y) = (-\frac{19}{8},\frac{4}{3} ) \)
D. \( (x,y) = (-\frac{19}{8},\frac{3}{4} ) \)

**Solution:**

  1. Multiply the second equation by 2 to align the coefficients of \(x\):

    2. \[ \begin{align*} 4x - 2y &= 8 \\ 4x + 6y &= 14 \end{align*} \]

  2. Subtract the first equation from the second equation:

    3. \[ (4x + 6y) - (4x - 2y) = 14 - 8 \implies 8y = 6 \implies y = \frac{6}{8} = \frac{3}{4} \]

  3. Substitute \(y = \frac{3}{4}\) into the first equation:

    4. \[ 4x - 2 \left(\frac{3}{4}\right) = 8 \implies 4x - \frac{3}{2} = 8 \implies 4x = 8 + \frac{3}{2} \implies 4x = \frac{16 + 3}{2} = \frac{19}{2} \implies x = \frac{19}{8} \]

  4. **Solution:** \(x = \frac{19}{8}\), \(y = \frac{3}{4}\)

Quadratic Simultaneous Equations

Quadratic simultaneous equations involve at least one quadratic equation, such as \(ax^2 + by^2 + cxy = d\). A common approach to solve these is to rearrange the linear equation to express one variable in terms of the other and then substitute this into the quadratic equation.

Example: Quadratic Equations

Solve the following system:

\[ \begin{align*} x + y &= 5 \\ x^2 + y^2 &= 13 \end{align*} \]

**Solution:**

  1. Rearrange the first equation to express \(x\) in terms of \(y\):

    2. \[ x = 5 - y \]

  2. Substitute \(x = 5 - y\) into the second equation:

    3. \[ (5 - y)^2 + y^2 = 13 \]

  3. Expand and simplify:

    4. \[ (5 - y)^2 + y^2 = 13 \implies 25 - 10y + y^2 + y^2 = 13 \implies 25 - 10y + 2y^2 = 13 \implies 2y^2 - 10y + 12 = 0 \]

  4. Divide through by 2:

    5. \[ y^2 - 5y + 6 = 0 \]

  5. Factorise to find \(y\):

    6. \[ (y - 2)(y - 3) = 0 \implies y = 2 \text{ or } y = 3 \]

  6. Substitute \(y\) values back into \(x = 5 - y\):
    • If \(y = 2\), then \(x = 5 - 2 = 3\)
    • If \(y = 3\), then \(x = 5 - 3 = 2\)
  7. **Solutions:** \((x, y) = (3, 2)\) or \((2, 3)\)

Solve the following system of quadratic equations:

\[ \begin{align*} x^2 + y^2 &= 25 \\ x - y &= 1 \end{align*} \]

A. \((x, y) = (3, 4)\) or \((4, 3)\)
B. \((x, y) = (5, 0)\) or \((0, 5)\)
C. \((x, y) = (4, 2)\) or \((2, 4)\)
D. \((x, y) = (4, 3)\) or \((-3, -4)\)

**Solution:**

  1. Rearrange the second equation to express \(x\) in terms of \(y\):

    2. \[ x = y + 1 \]

  2. Substitute \(x = y + 1\) into the first equation:

    3. \[ (y + 1)^2 + y^2 = 25 \]

  3. Expand and simplify:

    4. \[ (y^2 + 2y + 1) + y^2 = 25 \implies 2y^2 + 2y + 1 = 25 \implies 2y^2 + 2y - 24 = 0 \implies y^2 + y - 12 = 0 \]

  4. Factorise to find \(y\):

    5. \[ (y - 3)(y + 4) = 0 \implies y = 3 \text{ or } y = -4 \]

  5. Substitute \(y\) values back into \(x = y + 1\):
    • If \(y = 3\), then \(x = 3 + 1 = 4\)
    • If \(y = -4\), then \(x = -4 + 1 = -3\)
  6. **Solutions:** \((x, y) = (4, 3)\) or \((-3, -4)\)

Topic Review: Simultaneous Equations

In this topic, you have learned the following key concepts:

  • Definition of Simultaneous Equations: Simultaneous equations are a set of equations with multiple variables that are solved together. The solutions must satisfy all given equations simultaneously.
  • Linear Simultaneous Equations: These equations are of the form \(ax + by = c\). Common methods for solving them include:
    • Substitution Method: Solve one equation for one variable and substitute that expression into the other equation.
    • Elimination Method: Add or subtract equations to eliminate one variable and solve for the remaining variable.
  • Quadratic Simultaneous Equations: Involve at least one quadratic equation, such as \(ax^2 + by^2 + cxy = d\). Solve these by rearranging the linear equation to express one variable in terms of the other and substituting it into the quadratic equation.
  • Graphical Interpretation: The solution to simultaneous equations can also be interpreted graphically as the point(s) where the graphs of the equations intersect.
