Simultaneous Equations


Introduction to Simultaneous Equations

Simultaneous equations consist of a set of equations involving multiple variables which are solved together. The solutions must satisfy all the given equations simultaneously. This topic serves as a refresher from GCSE Maths, revisiting the fundamental concepts and methods used to solve such systems.

Linear Simultaneous Equations

Linear simultaneous equations are those where each equation is of the form \(ax + by = c\). Common methods for solving these include substitution and elimination.

Substitution Method:

In this method, you solve one of the equations for one variable and then substitute that expression into the other equation.

Example: Substitution

Solve the following system of equations:

\[ \begin{align*} x + y &= 5 \\ x - y &= 1 \end{align*} \]

**Solution:**

  1. Rearrange the first equation to express \(x\) in terms of \(y\):

    2. \[ x = 5 - y \]

  2. Substitute \(x = 5 - y\) into the second equation:

    3. \[ (5 - y) - y = 1 \implies 5 - 2y = 1 \implies -2y = 1 - 5 \implies -2y = -4 \implies y = 2 \]

  3. Substitute \(y = 2\) back into the first equation:

    4. \[ x + 2 = 5 \implies x = 5 - 2 \implies x = 3 \]

  4. **Solution:** \(x = 3\), \(y = 2\)

Elimination Method:

This method involves adding or subtracting equations to eliminate one of the variables, making it easier to solve for the remaining variable.

Example: Elimination

Solve the following system of equations:

\[ \begin{align*} 2x + 3y &= 7 \\ 4x - y &= 5 \end{align*} \]

**Solution:**

  1. Multiply the second equation by 3 to align the coefficients of \(y\):

    2. \[ \begin{align*} 2x + 3y &= 7 \\ 12x - 3y &= 15 \end{align*} \]

  2. Add the equations to eliminate \(y\):

    3. \[ (2x + 3y) + (12x - 3y) = 7 + 15 \implies 14x = 22 \implies x = \frac{22}{14} = \frac{11}{7} \]

  3. Substitute \(x = \frac{11}{7}\) into the first equation:

    4. \[ 2\left(\frac{11}{7}\right) + 3y = 7 \implies \frac{22}{7} + 3y = 7 \implies 3y = 7 - \frac{22}{7} = \frac{49 - 22}{7} = \frac{27}{7} \implies y = \frac{27}{21} = \frac{9}{7} \]

  4. **Solution:** \(x = \frac{11}{7}\), \(y = \frac{9}{7}\)

Quadratic Simultaneous Equations

Quadratic simultaneous equations involve at least one quadratic equation, such as \(ax^2 + by^2 + cxy = d\). A common approach to solve these is to rearrange the linear equation to express one variable in terms of the other and then substitute this into the quadratic equation.

Example: Quadratic Equations

Solve the following system:

\[ \begin{align*} x + y &= 5 \\ x^2 + y^2 &= 13 \end{align*} \]

**Solution:**

  1. Rearrange the first equation to express \(x\) in terms of \(y\):

    2. \[ x = 5 - y \]

  2. Substitute \(x = 5 - y\) into the second equation:

    3. \[ (5 - y)^2 + y^2 = 13 \]

  3. Expand and simplify:

    4. \[ (5 - y)^2 + y^2 = 13 \implies 25 - 10y + y^2 + y^2 = 13 \implies 25 - 10y + 2y^2 = 13 \implies 2y^2 - 10y + 12 = 0 \]

  4. Divide through by 2:

    5. \[ y^2 - 5y + 6 = 0 \]

  5. Factorise to find \(y\):

    6. \[ (y - 2)(y - 3) = 0 \implies y = 2 \text{ or } y = 3 \]

  6. Substitute \(y\) values back into \(x = 5 - y\):
    • If \(y = 2\), then \(x = 5 - 2 = 3\)
    • If \(y = 3\), then \(x = 5 - 3 = 2\)
  7. **Solutions:** \((x, y) = (3, 2)\) or \((2, 3)\)
