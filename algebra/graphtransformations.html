Graphical Representations of Functions

Graphs of functions provide a visual representation of mathematical relationships. Understanding these graphs is essential for solving equations and interpreting algebraic solutions. Below are some key types of functions and their graphical characteristics.

1. Linear Functions

Linear functions describe a straight line on a graph. They are represented by equations of the form:

\[ y = ax + b \]

Where \( a \) is the gradient of the line and \( b \) is the y-intercept.

Example: The function \( y = 2x + 3 \) has a gradient of 2 and a y-intercept of 3.

2. Modulus Functions

Modulus functions use the absolute value of a linear function. They are represented by:

\[ y = |ax + b| \]

The graph of a modulus function has a V-shape and reflects across the x-axis where the linear function crosses zero.

Example: The function \( y = |x - 2| \) reflects at \( x = 2 \).

Step-by-Step Guide to Drawing Modulus Graphs

  1. Identify the function: Start with the modulus function you want to graph, e.g., \( y = |2x - 4| \).
  2. Determine the critical point: Find where the expression inside the absolute value equals zero. Solve \( 2x - 4 = 0 \) to get \( x = 2 \).
  3. Evaluate function values: Choose points on either side of the critical point \( x = 2 \). Let's choose \( x = 0 \) and \( x = 4 \).
  4. Calculate corresponding \( y \)-values:
    • For \( x = 0 \): \( y = |2(0) - 4| = | - 4| = 4 \)
    • For \( x = 4 \): \( y = |2(4) - 4| = |8 - 4| = 4 \)
  5. Plot the points: The points to plot are \( (0, 4) \) and \( (4, 4) \). The critical point is \( (2, 0) \).
  6. Draw the V-shape: Connect the points smoothly to form a V-shape that opens upwards, with the vertex at the critical point \( (2, 0) \).

Example Graph

Note on Negative Absolute Value Functions

When the modulus function includes a negative sign, such as \( y = -|ax + b| \), the graph will form an upside-down V shape. This occurs because the negative sign inverts the values of the function, causing it to open downward instead of upward. The vertex of the upside-down V will be at the critical point, and the arms will extend downward as you move away from the vertex.

3. Quadratic Functions (Brief Overview)

Quadratic functions are polynomial functions of degree 2 and are briefly discussed here. They are represented by:

\[ y = ax^2 + bx + c \]

For more details on sketching quadratic functions, refer to the quadratic functions section previously covered.

4. Vertical and Horizontal Asymptotes

Vertical asymptotes occur where a function approaches infinity as \( x \) approaches a certain value. They are found by setting the denominator of a rational function to zero and solving:

\[ \text{Vertical Asymptote: } x = \text{value where denominator} = 0 \]

Horizontal asymptotes represent the behavior of the function as \( x \) approaches infinity. For rational functions:

\[ \text{Horizontal Asymptote: } y = \frac{\text{leading coefficient of numerator}}{\text{leading coefficient of denominator}} \]

Example: For the function \( y = \frac{2x}{x - 1} \), the vertical asymptote is \( x = 1 \) and the horizontal asymptote is \( y = 2 \).

5. Proportional Relationships

Proportional relationships are represented by direct or inverse proportions. The graph of a proportional relationship passes through the origin (for direct proportions) or has a hyperbolic shape (for inverse proportions).

Direct Proportionality: \( y = kx \)

Inverse Proportionality: \( y = \frac{k}{x} \)

Example: If \( y \) is directly proportional to \( x \) with \( k = 4 \), then \( y = 4x \). If \( y \) is inversely proportional to \( x \) with \( k = 12 \), then \( y = \frac{12}{x} \). Here, the blue line represents \( y = 4x \) and the green curve represents \( y = \frac{12}{x} \).

6. Intersection Points

Intersection points of graphs represent the solutions to equations where the graphs cross each other. To find these points, follow these steps:

  • Graph the equations: Plot both equations on the same set of axes.
  • Identify intersection points: Look for the x-values where the graphs intersect.
  • Calculate corresponding y-values: Substitute the identified x-values back into the original equations to find their corresponding y-values.

Example: To find the intersection of \( y = x^2 \) and \( y = x + 2 \), plot both functions on a graph. The points where they intersect are the solutions to the equations.

In this example, the intersection points occur at \( x = -1 \) and \( x = 2 \). Substituting these values back into the equations gives the corresponding \( y \)-values as follows:

  • For \( x = -1 \): \( y = (-1)^2 = 1 \), so one intersection point is \((-1, 1)\).
  • For \( x = 2 \): \( y = 2 + 2 = 4 \), so the other intersection point is \((2, 4)\).

By examining the graph, you will notice that the points of intersection correspond to the solutions of the simultaneous equations \( y = x^2 \) and \( y = x + 2 \). This demonstrates that the points of intersection are indeed the solutions to the simultaneous equations, and conversely, the solutions indicate where the graphs intersect.
